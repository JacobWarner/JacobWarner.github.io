Ethical Impact
of Automonous Vehicles

An Abstract

There is a stronger push than ever to develop self-driving cars, and the world is headed for an exciting new change in transportation. This opens new opportunities for ride-sharing companies like Uber and Lyft to deploy huge, efficient fleets of autonomous vehicles. The benefits of self-driving cars are obvious: they will be safer than human drivers because they make fewer mistakes, they can improve traffic conditions by communicating between each other to fill lanes, and they can save the time of their passengers by allowing them to do other things while the car drives for them. So why are people afraid of self-driving cars? These fears all stem from quite a few ethical and legal issues. First, there is a lack of responsibility that comes with self-driving cars. Yes, autonomous cars that are even just 10 percent safer than human drivers would save nearly 3000 lives a year, but if a human can’t be at fault when an autonomous vehicle crashes, who is there to blame? New policies will need to be put in place to determine fault and ease people’s minds. This is just one of many legal concerns surrounding the development of self-driving vehicles. The large ethical concern is how the car should make decisions during an accident - a “modern” variation of the trolley problem. How should the car choose between life during an accident? Should it aim for the least loss of life, or protect its passengers at all costs? Self-driving cars also have the potential to eliminate American jobs surrounding the car industry, especially if production is pushed to other countries and completed by German companies, who are currently leading the industry. Lastly, they eliminate the thrill of driving and could take away the uniqueness and creative differences between cars that humans use to express themselves.

At some point, autonomous vehicles must be deployed on the roads to collect valuable data to improve its driving, but this process could take years if only a few are let onto the road at a time. When will people be satisfied with the safety levels of autonomous vehicles like they are with those of auto-piloted planes? When will they feel as comfortable giving up control of the car to a machine as they do with a taxi driver?
